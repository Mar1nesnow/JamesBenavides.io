About

a little about my background, education, and where I've worked.

I'm an environmental scientist with a passion for understanding ecosystems through both data and lived experience.

In 2025, I earned dual degrees in Biology (Marine, Ecology, and Evolution) and Gender, Sexuality, and Women's Studies from Bowdoin College in Maine — an interdisciplinary foundation that shapes how I approach environmental work as both science and story.

My research experience spans sea star behavior, oyster aquaculture, and marine protist culturing, with experience designing, conducting, and presenting projects. With Texas Parks and Wildlife — Coastal Fisheries, I worked on permitting, public engagement, and fisheries sampling, gaining insight into applied conservation at the state level.

I currently serve as an AmeriCorps member at Whiterock Conservancy in Iowa, focused on land stewardship — invasive species removal, soil and water sampling, environmental education, and trail and campground maintenance.

I value preparation and safety in the field, but I also believe curiosity and connection to place are just as essential. Moving forward, I'm eager to deepen my skills in data analysis, programming, scientific communication, and grant writing alongside hands-on environmental work.

Field / Lab Skills

the tools, techniques, and field methods I bring to land stewardship, coastal fisheries, and marine research.

Land Stewardship
Restoring native prairie, woodland, and riparian habitats through Timber Stand Improvement (TSI) and Invasive Species Management projects.

This work involves assessing a habitat and deciding what is best for that space. Tackling invasive honeysuckle, for example, has been a large part of my work — but deciding to foliar spray vs. hack-and-squirt really depends on the time of year and what species surround the invasives. Taking down trees that appear healthy but are too dense requires an understanding of spacing that roughly separates a savanna from a woodland.

Prescribed Fire
Burning land to reduce woody undergrowth and invasive spread, and to aid in native plant recovery.

I was part of burn operations from planning through active ignition and post-burn assessment. These burns require strong communication — fire is an effective tool, but safety is always the top priority.

In my first year of burning in 2026, I burned over 500 acres of land. I've completed my L-180 and S-130 certifications, and my S-190 is currently in progress.

Fisheries Field Methods
Monitoring estuarine and offshore habitats — adapting to tides, weather, and dynamic conditions to collect reliable data in the field.

These field days solidified my ability to prepare thoroughly — you don't want to be two hours into a site and realize you forgot pencils or left a device uncharged.

I worked with oyster data collection, studied benthic biofouling, scanned for derelict crab traps using sonar, assessed juvenile fish populations, and worked with larger fish — including sharks — in offshore settings.

Public Engagement & Education

connecting communities to conservation through programs, surveys, and outreach — from elementary classrooms to boat ramps.

Education Programming
Facilitating hands-on field education for school groups and the general public, covering aquatic stream health, ornithology, and general ecology.

Adapting to different age groups is a great — if fun — challenge. I've taken the same lesson and delivered it to a group of 9th graders immediately after finishing with a group of 3rd graders, which requires real flexibility in how you frame concepts and hold attention.

I also developed and delivered an educational field program on harmful algal blooms for Texas Master Naturalists, complete with live water quality sampling demonstrations and instruction on HAB identification and proper response protocols.

Public speakingCommunity outreach
Public Surveying
Conducting creel surveys at boat ramps to learn about angler catch, bait, and trip experience — building a clearer picture of fishing behaviors and population pressures.

We recorded species, size, and catch numbers, which made for great practice in identification and handling of larger fish. The surveys also gave us a window into how people relate to and use these resources.

Being kind, approachable, and genuinely curious about someone's day consistently produced the best data. Good conversation and good science aren't mutually exclusive.

Outreach & Tabling
Representing organizations at public events — meeting people where they are and making conservation feel accessible and worth caring about.

Good tabling means knowing your material, reading your crowd, and adjusting accordingly — whether you're talking to college students or families with young kids. The approach that works for one group rarely works for the other.

Enthusiasm is contagious. Kindness makes people stop and stay. Those two things make for a great day of outreach.
